Introdcution

Children between the ages of 1-5 undergo a period of rapid growth in muscle, body tissues and the development of the brain. A large range of vital nutrients must be included within the smaller volumes of food they consume. The correct diet and approach to food issues are important factors in preventing many health and development problems in young children and in their future. Problems including obesity, faltering growth and stunting, iron deficiency, specific nutrient deficiencies, dental caries and developmental delay.

Establishing a regular meal pattern made up of 3 small balanced and varied meals including 2-3 nutritious snacks is recommended as the optimum way of ensuring a young child can meet their nutritional requirements. Foods offered should be nutrient dense, meet requirements and be varied and appealing.

Calorie Intake

Energy is provided by carbohydrates, protein and fat in the diet. The body stores energy to use on occasions when current dietary intake does not meet immediate energy needs. If energy intake exceeds the amount used, body fat stores will continue to increase and this will lead to overweight and obesity. If energy intake is below a child's energy requirements, all the body's energy stores will be used up and then growth and development will falter and there will be many other health problems such as increased susceptibility to infection.

Mineral Intake

Zinc plays a major role in the functioning of every organ in the body and helps the hormone insulin to control the body's energy. Zinc is also involved in the immune system, working with vitamin A, and in wound healing.

Iron is essential for several body functions and is needed to carry oxygen in the blood. A deficiency in iron can cause anaemia, which means that the blood cannot carry enough oxygen for all the body's needs. Therefore, children who are lacking in iron will most likely be lethargic rather than active. They are often pale and tired. They may suffer from poor growth and weight gain, find it difficult to concentrate and may not feel like eating much. A lack of iron may also affect brain development and behaviour making it difficult to do well at school later.

Calcium is required for building and maintaining bones, to carry messages from the brain to the body, for muscles to work and many other body functions. Most children under five get enough calcium as they drink plenty of milk. It is important to make sure that children who do not have breast milk or dairy products get enough calcium; for example by drinking soya drink fortified with calcium. A lack of calcium can lead to osteoporosis in later life.

Vitamin Intake

It is recommended that children get good amounts of vitamin A, C and D in their diets.

Vitamin D is only found in a few foods, such as oily fish and eggs. It's also added to some foods, such as fat spreads and breakfast cereals. The best source of vitamin D is summer sunlight on our skin. But it's important to keep your child's skin safe in the sun. Children shouldn't be out in the sun too long in hot weather. Remember to cover up or protect their skin before it turns red or burns. Young children should still have vitamin drops, even if they get out in the sun. Children aged 1 to 4 years old should be given a daily supplement containing 10μg of vitamin D.

Vitamin A is important for young children as it strengthens their immune system, can help their vision in dim light and keeps skin healthy. Good sources of vitamin A include dairy products, fortified fat spreads, carrots, sweet potatoes, swede, mangoes, spinach, cabbage and broccoli.

Vitamin C is important for your child's general health and immune system. It can also help their body absorb iron. Good sources of vitamin C include, oranges, kiwi fruit, strawberries, broccoli, tomatoes and peppers.

Protein Intake

Adequate protein intake is essential for growth, development and repair of body tissues in children. Foods naturally rich in protein include meat, fish, chicken, eggs, beans, pulses and nuts. At least 2 servings of a variety of protein rich foods should be provided per day. Vegetarian or vegan children will need up to 4 servings per day.

Carbohydrate Intake

Starchy carbohydrate food should be given at each meal and at least one snack during the day for children aged 1 to 5 years. Starchy carbs include bread, potatoes, rice, fortified breakfast cereals, cassava, chapatti, pasta and crackers. They are a good source of calories, provide B vitamins essential for growth and some fibre. A variety of different starchy foods should be used in the diet.

Fat Intake

Fat is an essential contributor to overall energy intake in the under 5's as it can provide a lot of calories in a small amount, without making the diet of a small child bulky. The healthiest options are olive, rapeseed and groundnut oils or fat spreads made from these. They are also a good source of vitamin A, D and E and can provide a good balance of omega 3 and 6 essential fatty acids.

Fats high in saturated fats such as butter, lard, dripping and some hard vegetable fats should be limited in use and used only occasionally.

Low fat, low calorie or 'diet' alternatives are not usually suitable for a rapidly growing child as they may not provide enough energy. Full fat milk is recommended as the main milk for children up to 5 years as the fat provides vital energy and fat-soluble vitamins. However, if a younger child is consistently eating a balanced diet and growing well semi -skimmed milk can be given as the main milk from 2 years and skimmed milk from 5 years.

Sugar Intake

Non-Milk Extrinsic Sugars include table sugar, soft drinks, fruit juices, confectionary, chocolate, cakes and biscuits. These foods should be restricted in amount and frequency. Excessive consumption is linked with overweight and obesity in children. Diets containing a high amount of NMES based foods are often high in energy but low in other essential nutrients. Sugars, excluding those naturally found in whole fruit and milk, should provide less than 5% of total energy in the diet.

Salt Intake

The main source of salt (also known as sodium chloride or referred to as sodium on nutrition labels) is from table salt, processed foods and takeaways. High salt intakes are linked with high blood pressure and kidney problems. It may also lead to excessive fluid intake which can aggravate bedwetting.
