Nutrition for 6-12 month olds

"Investing in early childhood nutrition is a surefire strategy. The returns are incredibly high." – Anne M.Mulcahy

Introdcution

Introducing your baby to solid foods should start when your baby is around 6 months old.

It is more important to get your baby use to the idea of food, as opposed to the quantity they eat to begin with. They will still be getting most of their nutrients from breast milk or first infant formula.

Begin by offering food at a time that suits you both. You'll be able to gradually increase the amount and variety of food your baby eats until they can eat small portions of the same food as the rest of the family.

Meals

When first weaning, start by mashing or pureeing your baby’s food. Mash or puree foods such as parsnip, potato, yam, sweet potato, carrots, apples or pear and allow to cool before feeding to your baby.

Keep feeding your baby breast milk or infant formula, but don't give them whole cows', goats' or sheep's milk as a drink until they are 1 year old.

Finger Food

Once your baby starts solid foods, encouraging them to have finger foods is good practise for feeding themselves.

Try grabbable bits of soft ripe banana or avocado. Pieces about the size of your own finger work well.

Always stay with your baby when they're eating in case they start to choke.

7 months

Once your baby is used to the foods above, they can have soft cooked meat such as chicken, mashed fish (boneless), pasta, noodles, toast, pieces of chapatti, lentils, rice and mashed hard-boiled eggs.

8-9 months

Aim to start feeding your baby 3 meals a day. The meals should consist of a mixture of soft finger foods, and mashed or chopped foods.

Adding variety to your baby’s diet is important. Incorporate fruit, vegetables, bread, rice, potatoes, meat, fish, beans and dairy products.

Mineral Intake

Iron Contributes to normal oxygen transport in the body and to normal cognitive development of children. Sources of iron can be found in red meat and cereals. Plant sources are lower in iron but tend to come along with vitamin C which aids absorption. Good sources include: greens, spinach, tofu, broccoli and lentils.

Zinc Contributes to the normal function of the immune system. Sources can be found in meat, fish, cheese, eggs and pulses.

Calcium is needed for normal growth and development of children’s bones. Sources can be found in dairy or fortified plant milks, cheese and green leafy vegetables.

Vitamin Intake

It is recommended that children get good amounts of vitamin A, C and D in their diets.

Vitamin D is needed for normal growth and development of children’s bones. It is made by the action of sunlight on the skin but in the UK not in enough quantity to support a baby’s rapid bone development. Food sources include oily fish and eggs.

Vitamin A contributes to the maintenance of normal vision, skin and immune system functioning. Sources include oily fish, cheese and butter. Carotenes is converted to vitamin A and can be sourced from dark green leafy vegetables, carrots, sweet potatoes and broccoli.

Vitamin C increases iron absorption and contributes to the normal function of the immune system. Sources include peppers, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, berries, oranges and kiwi fruit.

Vitamin B12 and B3 contributes to normal functioning of the nervous system and contributes to the normal function of the immune system. Folic acid contributes to the normal function of the immune system and normal blood formation. Green vegetables, brown rice, chick peas and fortified cereals are a good source of folic acid. The other B vitamins are found in whole grains, brown rice, bananas, beans, eggs, meat, poultry and fish.

Protein Intake

Adequate protein intake is essential for growth, development and repair of body tissues in children. Foods naturally rich in protein include meat, fish, chicken, eggs, beans and pulses.

Carbohydrate Intake

Starchy carbs include bread, potatoes, rice, fortified breakfast cereals, cassava, chapatti, pasta and crackers. They are a good source of calories, provide B vitamins essential for growth and some fibre. A variety of different starchy foods should be used in the diet.

Fat Intake

Fat is an essential contributor to overall energy intake for the baby as it can provide a lot of calories in a small amount, without making the diet of a small child bulky. The healthiest options are olive, rapeseed and groundnut oils or fat spreads made from these. They are also a good source of vitamin A, D and E and can provide a good balance of omega 3 and 6 essential fatty acids.

Fats high in saturated fats such as butter, lard, dripping and some hard vegetable fats should not be used at all.

Sugar Intake

Frequently consuming sugar-containing foods and drinks can lead to tooth decay, especially in-between meals. Avoid adding sugar to foods, as well as giving foods and drinks with added sugars, such as biscuits, cakes and fruit squash to your baby.

Salt Intake

Babies under one year should have less than 1g (less than ¼ tsp) of salt per day as their kidneys cannot cope with very much salt. Foods prepared at home should have no salt added.
