Nutrition for the first 6 months

"Our food should be our medicine and our medicine should be our food." – Hippocrates

Introdcution

Breastfeeding is usually recommended for the first 6 months of life to ensure babies are given the best start in life. Breast milk provides all the energy and nutrients that a baby needs, as well as growth factors and immunological components that help protect the baby from infection.

If a mother is unable to breastfeed, or chooses not to, then infant formula should be given as an alternative. Strict criteria on the composition of and permitted ingredients in infant formulas are set out by the European Commission to ensure that infant formulas contain recommended amounts of nutrients and that additions are carefully regulated. This means that the vitamin and mineral content of different brands of formula is very similar, although they may differ in the source of protein or the addition of novel ingredients.

Mineral Intake

During the first 6 months of life, infants can obtain sufficient iron from breastmilk or from an appropriate infant formula.

Vitamin Intake

Vitamins and minerals are micronutrients, the majority of which cannot by synthesised by the body and must be provided in the diet regularly. Some vitamins and minerals are more easily absorbed from human milk than from formula milk and are added to formula milks in higher amounts to compensate for this.

Vitamin D is essential for bone health in infancy, and breastfed babies under 6 months of age should receive sufficient vitamin D if their mother had a good status in pregnancy. Vitamin D is added to infant milks in the form of vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) and infant formula provides enough vitamin D for the first year of life.

Protein Intake

The two main types of milk protein found in infant formula are whey and casein. Breast milk contains predominantly whey protein (60% whey, 40% casein) and hence all first formulas contain a higher percentage of whey protein. Whey protein appears to be digested more easily than casein and several studies have found that casein empties from the stomach more slowly. There are a number of formulas available that are labelled as suitable for ‘hungrier’ babies and these contain slightly more protein which is predominantly casein. However, these formulas do not offer any additional nutrients or energy compared to standard first formulas.

Breastfeeding is associated with a lower risk of developing obesity in later life and one of the reasons that breast milk is considered to be protective is that breastfed babies tend to grow more slowly. The protein content of formula compared to breast milk is thought to be a factor contributing to the different growth patterns. As a result, a number of formula manufacturers have reduced the protein content of infant formulas to make it closer to breast milk. The long-term effects of lower versus higher protein content in formulas are currently being studied.

Carbohydrate Intake

Carbohydrates will be included in the breast milk or formula the baby drinks. Lactose is the main source of carbohydrate in all milk and is present in infant milks either from the milk base used or because it has been added back to a milk made up from its constituent parts. Most infant milks for sale in the UK have lactose as the main source of carbohydrate, but soy protein based formula have glucose syrups added and some milks contain maltodextrin.

Fat Intake

The fat component of human milk is highly variable and changes according to certain factors including the duration of feed, stage of lactation and the dietary habits of the mother. Fat supplies 50% of energy in infants who are exclusively breastfed and fats are added to infant milks to supply 50% of dietary energy.

Sugar Intake

Maltodextrin is easily digestible, being absorbed as rapidly as glucose in the body, and can be either moderately sweet or almost flavourless. It is commonly used as an ingredient in a wide variety of processed foods, particularly where bulk without sweetness is needed at low cost.

Salt Intake

Babies get salt through breast feeding and formula. Do not add any extra salt as their kidneys are too young to digest and break it down.
