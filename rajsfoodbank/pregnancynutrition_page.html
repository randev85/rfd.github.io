Introdcution

The basic principles of a healthy diet stay the same during pregnancy. You should still eat a diet that is based on wholegrain starchy foods that includes plenty of fruit and vegetables, moderate amounts of lean meat, fish and/or other protein sources such as eggs and pulses, and moderate amounts of dairy products, such as milk, yogurt and cheese. You should only eat limited amounts of foods and drinks that are high in fat and sugar.

Calorie Intake

Energy is provided by carbohydrates, protein and fat in the diet. The body stores energy to use on occasions when current dietary intake does not meet immediate energy needs. If energy intake exceeds the amount used body fat stores will continue to increase and this will lead to overweight and obesity.

Mineral Intake

Zinc is a component of various enzymes that help maintain structural integrity of proteins and help regulate gene expression. Getting enough Zinc is particularly important for the rapid cell growth that occurs during pregnancy. Zinc can be found in lean meat, wholegrain cereals, milk, seafood, legumes and nuts.

Pregnancy can deplete a motherâ€™s iron stores. It is important to have the right intake of iron to help build and maintain these stores. Low iron levels in early pregnancy have been linked to premature birth and low birth weight.

Red meat is the best source of iron as well as also being a good source of protein and zinc. Iron can also be found in leafy green vegetables, legumes and iron-enriched breakfast cereals.

Adding foods and drinks rich in vitamin C to a meal will increase the amount of iron the body absorbs.

Calcium is vital for making your babyâ€™s bones and teeth. Breakfast cereals, dairy product, dried fruit, almonds, tofu and green leafy vegetables are other good sources of calcium.

Calcium in combination with Vitamin D are complementary in maintaining bone health.

Iodine is needed in very small but essential amounts by the human body. Iodine is essential to the production of thyroid hormone, which regulates body temperature, metabolic rate, reproduction, growth, blood cell production and nerve and muscle function. Thyroid hormone is produced in the thyroid gland, which is in the neck.

Vitamin Intake

Folate is a B vitamin that is needed for healthy growth and development. Its needs are increased during pregnancy for normal growth of the unborn baby. Adequate folate intake helps to prevent birth defects in the baby, such as spina bifida.

You need vitamin D to keep your bones healthy and to provide your baby with enough vitamin D for the first few months of its life. Vitamin D regulates the amount of calcium and phosphate in the body, and these are needed to help keep bones and teeth healthy.

The need for vitamin C is increased in pregnancy due to larger blood volume in the mother and the growth of the unborn baby. Vitamin C is important for the formation of collagen which is especially important in blood vessels.

The Reference Nutrient Intake is set at 0.75g of protein per kilogram per day in adults.

For Example:

If an adult weighs 60kg, they will need:
60 x 0.75g/d = 45g of protein a day

Protein requirements increase in pregnancy and lactation. It is recommended to increase protein by 6g per day during pregnancy, by 11g per day during the first 6 months of lactation and by 8g per day after the fist 6 months of lactation.

Protein Intake

To reduce the risk of getting a foodborne illness during pregnancy, there are certain things you may need to give up or take care with when you're pregnant.

Avoid eating pate because on the rare occasion, pate can contain listeria bacteria, and this can be harmful to yourself and your baby.

Stay away from raw or cured meats as they can carry bacteria that cause food born illness. Red meats should be cooked thoroughly to at least medium-well to minimise food born illness.

High levels of Vitamin A are not recommended during pregnancy and therefore, liver should be avoided during this time.

Avoid shark, marlin and swordfish, as they can contain high levels of mercury that can harm your babyâ€™s developing nervous system. Tuna can also contain mercury, so you should not eat more than two tuna steaks or four cans a week. Avoid eating lightly cooked wild fish as it sometimes contains parasitic worms. Raw shellfish is a no go as it carries a risk of food poisoning. Shellfish should only be consumed if it is alive before the cooking process, is from a safe supplier, and it is thoroughly cooked.

Fish is great for your babyâ€™s health and development, but you should not eat more than two portions of oily fish a week because they can contain pollutants, which may harm your baby.

Unpasteurised milk and foods containing it can be dangerous to you and the baby as they can contain bacteria that are harmful to the body.

Soft cheeses made with unpasteurised milk should also be avoided. Pasteurised soft cheeses like cream cheese, cottage cheese and mozzarella are safe.

Hen eggs are a good source of protein for pregnant women. More than 90% of UK eggs are produced with the highest hygiene standards and carry the British Lion mark. This is because the British Lion Code of Practice ensures high standards of food safety and this scheme has drastically reduced the presence of salmonella in UK eggs. Eggs should be avoided if they are UK non-lion eggs, eggs from outside the UK or no-hen eggs (e.g. duck, quail or goose eggs). If you are going to eat these then make sure they are thoroughly cooked until the whites and yolks are solid to prevent the risk of salmonella food poisoning.

Carbohydrate Intake

Starchy foods are an important source of energy, some vitamins and fibre, and help fill you up without containing too many calories. They include bread, potatoes, breakfast cereals, rice, pasta, noodles, maize, millet, oats, yams and cornmeal.

Instead of refined starchy food, choose wholegrain or higher fibre options such as whole wheat pasta, brown rice or simply leaving the skins on potatoes.

Fat Intake

Fats are very high in calories and eating them too often can make you put on weight. Having too much saturated fat can increase the amount of cholesterol in the blood, which increases the chance of developing heart disease.

It is recommended that healthy fats are consumed on a day to day basis. Choose monosaturated fats like vegetable oil, olive oil, coconut oil and avocado instead of saturated fats from fried food and processed food.

Sugar Intake

High sugar intake during pregnancy may increase the risk of allergy and allergic asthma in the offspring, according to an early study led by Queen Mary University of London involving almost 9,000 mother-child pairs.

Sugary foods and drinks are often high in calories which can contribute to weight gain.

Eat fruits as they contain natural sugar and fibre that are nutrient rich and take longer to digest.

Salt Intake

Salt plays an important role in helping to regulate and maintain your body fluid. It is used by all the cells in your body to function and is also needed for transmitting messages between the brain and the rest of the body.

The well-documented problem with high salt intake is its association with blood pressure. Salt affects the kidneys, causing the body to retain water. This extra fluid results in greater blood volume, which causes blood pressure to rise.

A low salt diet during pregnancy can help to keep blood pressure within a healthy range, reducing the risks of stroke and heart problems.
